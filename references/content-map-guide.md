# Content Map Guide

This file defines the professional standard for building a podcast content map. Read it in full before starting Step 4. It covers: the tension curve, the Ira Glass framework, the Gold Quote System, the content map format template, and format-specific writing rules.

---

## 1. The Tension Curve (draw this first — before anything else)

**Before writing a single segment, map the emotional arc of the entire episode.** This is non-negotiable.

Draw it as a simple curve with three points:

```
Tension Curve:

Act 1 baseline: [What emotional state does the listener start in?]
                [What question gets planted in the first 3 minutes?]

Act 2 peak:     [What is the hardest, most uncomfortable moment?]
                [What assumption gets broken here?]
                [Why can't the listener look away?]

Act 3 landing:  [What does the listener leave with?]
                [NOT an answer — a new way of seeing the question]
                [The feeling should be: "I need to think about this"]
```

**The arc must have genuine movement.** If Act 1 and Act 3 feel emotionally similar, the episode has no arc — redesign it.

### Common arc failures

- **Linear progression** (A → B → C, each step slightly deeper) — this is a lecture, not a podcast
- **Resolution arc** (problem → analysis → solution) — this is a self-help article, not a podcast
- **Agreement arc** (we all feel this → here's why → we agree it's hard) — this is a support group, not a podcast

### What a real arc looks like

- Comfort → Discomfort → New discomfort (the answer creates a harder question)
- Certainty → Doubt → Productive uncertainty (the listener ends less sure, but more awake)
- Personal → Universal → Personal again (zoom out to the pattern, zoom back in to what it means for you)

## 2. The Ira Glass Framework

Ira Glass (creator of This American Life) developed the most authoritative framework for podcast storytelling.

**Two fundamental building blocks:**

1. **Anecdote**: A sequence of events unfolding in time, where each event raises a question that pulls the listener forward. Not a summary — a scene. Something is happening, and the listener doesn't know how it ends.

2. **Moment of Reflection**: After the story, explain why it matters — what larger truth it reveals. This is the "why does this matter" beat. Without it, the story is just a sequence of events.

**The golden rule**: Every story needs a Moment of Reflection. Every Moment of Reflection needs a story to earn it.

**Cross-time clustering**: Do not follow the chronological order of the chat log. The chat log is raw material — your job is to find the underlying architecture. Ask: what is this conversation *really* about? Cluster by emotional and intellectual resonance, not by when things were said.

---

## 3. Information Density Standard

Professional podcasts run at 150–160 words per minute. Every minute of airtime must carry **at least one new piece of information, one emotional shift, or one perspective change**. If a minute passes without any of these, that content should be cut.

Apply this test to every segment: **"If a listener skipped this 2-minute block, what would they miss?"** If the answer is "not much," compress or cut.

**Signs of low information density (cut these):**
- Two speakers agreeing with each other at length
- Restating what was just said in different words
- Extended personal anecdotes that don't connect to the episode's core question
- Transitions that take more than 15 seconds
- Any exchange that could be summarized as "they talked about how hard X is"

## 4. Gold Quote System

Each topic in the content map gets 2–3 gold quotes. These are the lines that could appear on a quote card, be read aloud as a teaser, or anchor the episode's emotional core. There are two types — use both.

### Type 1 — Original quotes (selected from the chat log)

Not every line in the chat is a gold quote. Most chat messages are functional, conversational, or half-formed. A genuine original quote must meet **at least two** of these criteria:

- It captures a feeling or insight that many people have but rarely articulate
- It contains a specific, concrete image or detail (not a vague generality)
- It has a rhythm or compression that makes it memorable
- It reveals something about the speaker's inner world, not just their opinion
- It would make a listener stop and think "I've never heard it put that way"

**Wrong** (functional chat language, not a gold quote):
> "yeah I think the problem is that nobody really cares about indie music anymore"

**Right** (specific, compressed, emotionally true):
> "feeling invisible hurts more than losing money"

### Type 2 — Crafted quotes (AI-generated, inspired by the chat)

When the chat contains a powerful idea but no one said it in a quotable way, craft a quote that distills the spirit of what was discussed.

Rules:
- **Grounded in the chat**: The idea must come directly from what was actually discussed — not invented from thin air
- **Original writing**: Draw inspiration from the craft of poetry, literary nonfiction, and great podcast moments — but never copy or closely paraphrase existing works. Every word must be your own.
- **Compression over explanation**: A gold quote says in 10 words what a paragraph would take to explain
- **Concrete over abstract**: Prefer images and specifics over concepts and generalities
- **Earned, not decorative**: The quote must feel like it belongs to this conversation, not like a generic inspirational line

Craft influences to draw from (for style, not content):
- The compression of Mary Oliver, Ocean Vuong, or Maggie Nelson
- The plainspoken precision of Ira Glass or David Sedaris
- The emotional directness of great podcast cold opens (Serial, S-Town, This American Life)
- The aphoristic quality of great interview moments (Terry Gross, Krista Tippett)

**Label every quote clearly:**
- `[Original]` — exact words from the chat log, speaker attributed
- `[Crafted]` — AI-generated distillation, labeled as "crafted from [speaker]'s idea" or "crafted from the group's discussion"

Never present a crafted quote as if it were something someone actually said.

### Gold Quote Test (mandatory — every crafted quote must pass at least one)

**Test 1 — The counter-intuitive flip**: Does the quote say something that sounds wrong at first, but becomes obviously right after a second? If the listener's first reaction is "wait, that's not right… oh, actually it is" — it passes.

**Test 2 — The concrete image**: Does the quote contain a specific, sensory image rather than an abstract concept? Not "choices are hard" but "you're in love with someone who's never had a bad Monday." If you can picture it — it passes.

**Test 3 — The unsaid feeling**: Does the quote name something the listener has felt but never had words for? The test: would a listener screenshot this and send it to a friend saying "this is exactly it"? If yes — it passes.

**If a crafted quote fails all three tests, cut it.** A weak quote is worse than no quote — it signals that the episode has nothing to say.

**Examples:**

❌ Fails all three: "That job didn't change. Your perspective on it did." — explains a concept, no image, no flip, no unsaid feeling.

✅ Passes Test 2 + 3: "You fell in love with a version of that job that's never had a bad Monday." — concrete image (Monday), names the feeling of idealizing the unchosen option.

✅ Passes Test 1 + 3: "The offer isn't better than your job. It's just newer than your disappointments." — counter-intuitive (reframes "better" as "newer"), names something people feel but don't say.

## 6. Format-Specific Writing Rules

### Roundtable

Every guiding question must be designed to produce disagreement. If both speakers agree on everything, the episode has no tension. Build in at least one "collision question" per act — a question where the speakers' different life experiences will produce genuinely different answers.

### Deep Interview

Use "funnel questioning" — broad to specific, facts to feelings, past to future. The best question is often "what were you feeling in that moment — not what were you thinking?" Feelings are specific; thoughts are general.

### Narrative Documentary

Narration should be visual — describe a scene, don't summarize. Use present tense to create immediacy. Every narration block should end with a question, not a statement.

### Solo / Monologue

The opening must create resonance with a question or scene the listener can feel — don't lead with your thesis. The thesis, if there is one, belongs in Act 3.
